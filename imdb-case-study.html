Project Overview
The Problem
Worth Solving

IMDb is the world's largest film database — trusted by 200M+ users, backed by Amazon, and sitting on the most comprehensive entertainment dataset ever assembled. Yet it is losing the generation that will define the next decade of media consumption.

Gen Z discovers films through TikTok's FYP. They manage watchlists in Notes apps. They share opinions on Letterboxd. They find streaming availability on JustWatch. IMDb — theoretically the single platform that could serve all these needs — gets used only to check ratings, then abandoned.

This project asks a simple question: What if IMDb actually designed for the way people experience film?

The redesign is not about cosmetics. It is a fundamental rethinking of IMDb's product strategy — from a database you query to a platform that understands you, discovers with you, and helps you decide what to watch in under 60 seconds.

The result: a full UX sprint covering research, personas, information architecture, user flows, and a production-grade interactive prototype across desktop and mobile.

02
UX Research
What's Broken
& Why It Matters

A combination of heuristic evaluation, competitive analysis, and secondary research revealed eight core problems — each mapped to a severity level and a design response.

Critical

Discovery is Dead

Users can't find what to watch next. No mood-based filtering, no intelligent recommendations, no serendipity. IMDb functions as a catalog you already know how to use — not a guide to what you don't know yet.

Hick's Law violation
Too many paths, no clear entry
Critical

Cognitive Overload on Every Page

Movie pages surface cast, crew, trivia, parental guides, user reviews, awards, and technical specs simultaneously — without hierarchy, grouping, or progressive disclosure. Miller's Law is violated on every screen.

Miller's Law + Gestalt
No visual grouping or hierarchy
Critical

The Decision Paradox

IMDb's core job-to-be-done: help me decide what to watch. Yet there is no clear "worth watching" verdict, no streaming availability per region, no single CTA. Users leave IMDb to Google the answer.

Peak-End Rule failure
Journey ends in frustration
Critical

Aggressive, Disruptive Advertising

IMDb's ad density is among the highest of any media platform. Ads interrupt reading, hijack scroll, and break mobile layouts — alienating exactly the premium users IMDb's advertisers want to reach.

Aesthetic-Usability Effect
Perceived quality collapses
Moderate

Zero Meaningful Personalization

The homepage is identical for a first-time visitor and a 10-year power user. Ratings and watchlist data are collected but barely leveraged. The platform knows you but acts like it doesn't.

Recognition over Recall
No personalized shortcuts
Moderate

No Community or Social Layer

Reviews exist in isolation. There is no way to follow critics you trust, see what friends are watching, or discover films through social proof. Letterboxd has built its entire competitive advantage on this gap.

Social Proof principle
Missing entirely from product
Moderate

Mobile is an Afterthought

The mobile app compresses desktop content rather than rethinking it. Dense tables, small tap targets, horizontal overflow, and a bottom nav that doesn't match mental models. Gen Z users abandon on first friction.

Fitts's Law failure
Small targets, poor reach zones
Low

Dated Visual Identity

The yellow-black palette carries connotations from 2005. Gen Z describes IMDb as "functional but ugly" and "what my parents use." The brand has zero cultural currency with the demographic that will define the next decade.

Aesthetic-Usability Effect
Negative brand perception
"IMDb is data-rich but experience-poor. It knows everything about every film — and almost nothing about the person watching it."
— Hisham Diab, Research Synthesis

Visual Language

Every design decision is grounded in the research — not aesthetic preference alone. The system had to feel cinematic, modern, and culturally credible to Gen Z while remaining accessible to all user types.

Rationale: Cormorant Garamond brings editorial gravitas — the kind you see in film magazine mastheads. Outfit provides clean readability for UI text. DM Mono gives technical precision to data labels. Three fonts, three purposes, zero overlap.

Key Design Decisions
UX Decision · Discovery

Mood Picker as Primary Home CTA

Rather than genre dropdown or search, the primary entry point to discovery is emotional. Users select a mood — Bittersweet, Thrilling, Late Night — and receive a curated feed instantly. This reduces Hick's Law cognitive load from 20+ genre options to 6 emotional categories.

Hick's Law · Emotional Design
UI Decision · Film Page

Sticky Quick Decision Box

A persistent sidebar widget showing: IMDb rating, runtime, age rating, audience verdict percentage, and streaming availability. Answers the user's core question — "Should I watch this? Where?" — without any scrolling. Reduces the "leave to Google" behaviour pattern.

Peak-End Rule · Fitts's Law
UX Decision · Engagement

Tabbed Movie Page (not Scroll)

Information is reorganised into five progressive tabs: Overview, Cast, Reviews, Media, Details. Each tab serves a specific user intent. This directly addresses the cognitive overload problem — no user is ever confronted with more information than they sought.

Miller's Law · Progressive Disclosure
UX Decision · Retention

Spotify-Style Smart Playlists

Watchlists become intentional, named, shareable playlists with custom covers generated from film posters. "Date Night 🍿", "Classics I Owe Myself", "Horror Season". This transforms a forgotten list into a personal curatorial project — dramatically increasing feature usage and retention.

Endowment Effect · Social Proof
UI Decision · Mobile

Horizontal Mood Scroll on Mobile

The mobile Mood Picker is a horizontally scrollable row of chips — always visible, always accessible, never requiring a tap to reveal. Following TikTok's content-type row pattern which Gen Z users already understand intuitively. Familiar mental model, new context.

Jakob's Law · Recognition over Recall
UI Decision · Visual Identity

Cinematic Grain + Monochrome Palette

A subtle noise texture overlay on hero images gives the UI a filmic quality reminiscent of actual cinema. The strict monochromatic palette with a single gold accent creates the editorial restraint associated with Criterion, A24, and premium film culture — brands Nour actively aspires to.

Aesthetic-Usability Effect

Key Learnings
01

Emotion is a Valid Navigation System

The insight that mood should be the primary entry to discovery — not genre or search — came from observing how people actually talk about wanting to watch something. Nobody says "I want a genre-drama film." They say "I want something that makes me cry" or "I need something light." Design should meet users in their natural language.

02

The Competition Gap is a Design Opportunity

Running a structured competitive audit revealed that every competitor excels at one thing and fails at everything else. IMDb's actual competitive moat — its database — is underutilised. The design opportunity was not to build something new, but to use what already exists in a smarter, more human way.

03

One Clear Question per Screen

The biggest structural failure of the current IMDb is asking users to answer multiple questions simultaneously on every screen. The redesign enforces a single primary question per view: "What do you feel?" on Discover, "Should I watch this?" on Film Page, "What have I watched?" on Library. Clarity of purpose drives engagement.

04

Visual Identity is a Product Decision, not a Cosmetic One

The shift to a monochromatic, editorial palette is not aesthetic preference — it is a deliberate positioning decision. The gold/black IMDb palette signals "database tool." The new palette signals "cultural institution for serious film lovers." Brand perception directly affects willingness to engage, share, and pay.
